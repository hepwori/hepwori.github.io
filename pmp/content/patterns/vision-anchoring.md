## Context

Products rarely succeed on functional merit alone. Especially in complex organizations and competitive markets, internal teams and customers alike orient around stories—narratives that explain why a product matters and what future it enables.

A compelling vision gives coherence to effort. Yet, many teams anchor their storytelling narrowly around features, metrics, or internal success criteria. The product becomes detached from the transformation it is meant to serve.

## Problem

A product narrative that centers only on itself fails to create emotional or strategic pull. Stakeholders, customers, and teams all interpret the product's purpose differently. Without an anchoring story—one that situates the product inside a broader journey—momentum dissipates.

The absence of anchoring produces two failure modes:

1. **Feature Myopia:** framing value in terms of what the product *does* rather than what it *changes*.
2. **Narrative Drift:** teams invent inconsistent stories that fragment meaning and weaken alignment.

## Example

At Apigee, the API management company, the sales narrative didn't begin with "API gateways" or "traffic management." It began with digital transformation. The story went like this:

> *"In order to survive in the modern world, enterprises are undergoing digital transformation. The strongest future companies are those that transform most successfully. A big part of digital transformation is a robust API program. And a big part of doing that properly is… API management."*

Apigee wasn't selling APIs; it was selling the future its customers wanted to inhabit. The product was positioned as an enabler of that transformation. That framing created alignment across sales, marketing, and product, and gave internal teams a shared sense of purpose beyond feature delivery.

## Forces

- **Cognitive economy:** Audiences remember stories, not architectures.
- **Identity resonance:** Customers adopt products that reinforce their own aspirational narrative.
- **Abstraction gap:** Product builders speak in systems; audiences think in outcomes.
- **Internal alignment:** Without a shared vision story, functions interpret value differently.
- **Narrative decay:** Over time, stories drift unless actively maintained.

## Navigation Strategies

Anchor the product in the customer's transformation story—a credible, emotionally resonant narrative that situates the product as the means to an end they already value.

- Begin every strategic narrative with the world the customer wants to create, not the product being built.
- Define the transformation: what changes when this product succeeds?
- Align internal language around that story so every function can translate their work back to it.
- Keep the story alive: revisit, test, and evolve it as markets shift and customer identity changes.
- Ensure narrative coherence between what's promised externally and what's practiced internally.

## Consequences

- The organization rallies around a shared story of purpose and value.
- Teams align on outcomes rather than outputs.
- Sales, product, and marketing messages converge, increasing credibility and emotional pull.
- If misused, vision anchoring can become empty theater—aspirational language detached from delivery.

## Known Uses

- Apigee's "digital transformation" framing that tied API management to strategic survival.
- Salesforce's "customer success platform" linking CRM to an aspirational business identity.
- Amazon's "customer obsession" as a unifying narrative transcending specific products.
